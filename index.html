Robotics readiness and planning

Strong robotics programs require more than strong robots.

RoboShift helps schools understand whether their robotics programs are ready to grow, where hidden weaknesses may be limiting success, and what practical steps should come next.

What is RoboShift?

A decision-support platform for school robotics and AI programs.

RoboShift looks beyond equipment purchases and competition results to assess the conditions that determine whether a robotics program can reach students, support teachers, and remain effective over time.

01

Assess the whole program

RoboShift examines leadership, teacher capacity, curriculum, equipment, student access, sustainability, and readiness for robotics connected to AI.

02

Identify what is holding it back

The platform highlights strengths, priority gaps, evidence gaps, and patterns that may not be visible when each part of the program is considered separately.

03

Turn results into action

Schools receive tailored recommendations and a phased roadmap that can be developed into a practical 30, 60, and 90-day action plan.

Why RoboShift was created

A program can look successful and still be vulnerable.

Schools often invest in robotics because they want students to build, code, collaborate, compete, and prepare for emerging technologies. Yet buying equipment does not guarantee that a program is educationally meaningful, widely accessible, or sustainable.

A program may depend on one teacher, reach only a small group of students, operate separately from classroom learning, or continue purchasing technology without a clear plan for maintenance, progression, and long-term use.

RoboShift was created to make those implementation conditions visible before schools invest more time, funding, and equipment. It helps decision-makers determine what is already working, what needs attention, and which investments are most likely to strengthen the program.

The central question
Is this robotics program ready to make its next investment count?

RoboShift shifts the conversation from simply asking what schools should buy to examining what they are ready to implement, sustain, and expand.

Before you begin

Assess the program you have now, not the program you hope to have.

The audit contains 28 questions and normally takes about 8–10 minutes. Answer each question based on what is currently in place across the school or organization.

Honest responses produce the most useful recommendations. Selecting “Not sure” is appropriate when evidence is not currently available.
